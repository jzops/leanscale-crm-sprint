## Week 5: Tactical Playbooks

### Monday — Lead Scoring Setup Tips

```
Most lead scoring models are built by marketers guessing what sales cares about.

That's why most lead scoring models get ignored.

Here's how we set up lead scoring in every CRM sprint — and why reps actually trust it:

Step 1: Interview your 3 best reps.
Ask: "What makes you pick up the phone?"
(It's never "downloaded a whitepaper.")

Step 2: Score on behavior, not demographics.
What matters:
→ Visited pricing page (3x in a week)
→ Opened 5+ emails in 14 days
→ Replied to outbound
→ Attended a demo or webinar

What doesn't matter (as much as you think):
→ Job title
→ Company size
→ Industry

Step 3: Set ONE threshold.
Not "hot, warm, cold." Just: "Call this person now."

Simple scoring models get used.
Complex ones get turned off in month two.

Step 4: Review monthly.
Pull closed-won deals. Did the scoring predict them?
If not, adjust. If yes, leave it alone.

Lead scoring should make your reps faster, not give marketing a vanity metric.

Build it for the people who actually use it.
```

### Wednesday — Dashboard Design Principles

```
I've built 200+ CRM dashboards.

The ones that actually get used all have the same 5 things in common:

1. ONE number at the top.
   Total pipeline. Revenue to goal. Deals closing this month.
   Pick one. Make it huge. Make it impossible to ignore.

2. No more than 6 widgets.
   Every widget you add dilutes attention.
   If everything is important, nothing is.

3. Filters that match how your team thinks.
   "My deals" vs. "Team deals."
   "This month" vs. "This quarter."
   Don't make them click 4 dropdowns to see their pipeline.

4. One action-oriented report.
   "Deals with no activity in 14 days."
   "Leads assigned but not contacted."
   Something that makes someone DO something — not just look at a chart.

5. No pie charts.
   I will die on this hill.
   Bar charts. Tables. Funnels. Anything but pie charts.
   Your VP of Sales should not need to estimate angles to understand pipeline.

The best dashboard is the one your team checks every morning without being asked.

Design for habit, not for board meetings.
```

### Friday — Data Migration Checklist

```
We've migrated 30+ companies between CRMs.

Every clean migration follows the same checklist. Stealing it costs you nothing. Skipping it costs you months.

THE CRM DATA MIGRATION CHECKLIST:

Before you touch anything:
□ Export everything from the old system (full backup, every object)
□ Document every active integration
□ Map old fields to new fields (in a spreadsheet, not in your head)
□ Identify your "source of truth" for each data type

The cleanup (do this BEFORE migration, not after):
□ Deduplicate contacts (you have more dupes than you think)
□ Delete dead leads (no activity in 12+ months = gone)
□ Standardize formats (phone numbers, states, company names)
□ Verify email addresses (free tools exist — use them)

The migration itself:
□ Contacts and companies first
□ Then deals and pipelines
□ Then activities and notes
□ Then attachments (last, because they're slowest)

After migration:
□ Spot-check 50 random records
□ Verify all automations fire correctly
□ Confirm integrations are connected
□ Run a parallel system for 2 weeks minimum
□ Get sign-off from 3 actual users (not just the admin)

Print this. Tape it to your monitor. Thank me later.

Cybersecurity companies have the worst CRMs in tech.

Your sales cycle is long. 6-9 months is normal.
Your buyer committee is huge. CISO, CTO, IT Director, procurement.

What I see in every cybersecurity CRM I audit:

→ No multi-threading tracking (you're selling to 5 people and tracking 1)
→ Security questionnaires and compliance docs buried in email (not attached to the deal)
→ Pipeline that looks full but is actually stuck

The fix for cybersecurity CRM:

1. Build stages around buyer commitment, not your activity.
   "Champion identified" > "Technical validation complete" > "Security review passed" > "Procurement engaged"

2. Track every stakeholder on every deal.
   Contact roles. Influence mapping. Last activity per person.

3. Attach compliance artifacts to the deal record.
   SOC 2, pen test results, security questionnaires — all in one place.

4. Add a "days in stage" alert.
   If a deal sits for 30+ days, something is wrong. Surface it.

Cybersecurity sales is complex. Your CRM shouldn't make it harder.

Day 4: Automations
→ Lead assignment (round robin or territory)
→ Slack notification for new leads
→ "Deal going cold" alert at 14 days no activity

Day 5: Integrations
→ Email sync
→ Calendar sync
→ One Slack channel for deal alerts

Day 6: Dashboard
→ Total pipeline (one number, big)
→ Deals by stage
→ Activity by rep
→ Stale deals list

Day 7: Test
→ Have 2-3 reps use it for real
→ Fix what's broken
→ Remove what's confusing

That's a working CRM in a week.

Not perfect. Working.

Perfect comes later. Working comes now.
```

### Evergreen 7: Stop Customizing, Start Using

```
The most dangerous phrase in CRM:

"Can we customize it to..."

Customize it to match our process.
Customize it to capture this one edge case.
Customize it to generate this report nobody reads.

Every customization adds complexity.
Complexity adds training time.
Training time reduces adoption.

The best CRM implementations I've done have the LEAST customization.

Default fields. Standard objects. Out-of-the-box pipelines (with small tweaks).

The companies with 90%+ adoption? They kept it simple.

The companies with 30% adoption? They customized everything.

Correlation isn't causation. But after 50+ implementations, I'm pretty confident:

Simple systems get used.
Complex systems get abandoned.

Before you customize, ask: "Will a rep use this — or is this for someone else's report?"

If it's for the report, find a simpler way.
```

### Evergreen 8: The Monday Morning Test

```
Here's how I judge whether a CRM implementation worked:

The Monday Morning Test.

It's 8:00 AM on Monday. Your rep opens their laptop. What do they do first?

Option A: Open the CRM to see their pipeline, check what's closing this week, and plan their calls.

Option B: Open a spreadsheet, check their email, ask their manager what to focus on, and then grudgingly open the CRM to log last week's activity.

If the answer is B, the CRM failed.

Not the platform. Not the license. The implementation.

A good CRM is the first app your reps open in the morning.

Not because they're forced to. Because it's the fastest way to figure out what to do next.

That's the bar. That's the test.

Does your CRM pass it?
```

### Evergreen 9: The Reporting Trap

```
Here's a pattern I see with every new CRM client:

"We need 30 reports on Day 1."

No, you don't.

You need 3.

1. Pipeline by stage (How much do we have? Where is it?)
2. Activity by rep (Who's doing the work?)
3. Deals closing this month (What's real?)

Three reports. That's your operating system.

Everything else — conversion rates, lead source attribution, cohort analysis, win rate by segment — all of that can wait.

Not because it's unimportant. Because you can't build those reports until you have clean data. And you don't have clean data until your team has been using the CRM consistently for 60-90 days.

Reporting is a lagging indicator of CRM health.

Build adoption first. Reports second.

I've seen too many companies build 40 dashboards in month one, realize the data is garbage in month three, and throw the whole thing out in month six.

Start with 3 reports. Add more when the data earns your trust.
```

### Evergreen 10: The Handoff Nobody Talks About

```
The most important moment in any CRM implementation isn't go-live.

It's the handoff.

When the implementation team leaves and your internal team takes over.

This is where 80% of CRM projects start to decay.

The consultants had context. Your team doesn't.
The consultants knew why that workflow exists. Your team just sees it break.
The consultants built it. Your team inherited it.

Here's how we prevent the handoff from killing the project:

1. Documentation that a new hire could follow
   Not a 90-page manual. A 5-page guide: "Here's what exists, here's why, here's how to change it."

2. A recorded walkthrough
   30-minute Loom video. Show every automation, every dashboard, every integration. Your future self will thank you.

3. An internal CRM owner
   One person who "owns" the system. Not part-time. Not "also does marketing ops." One person.

4. A 30-day check-in after handoff
   We do this on every sprint. Come back in 30 days. See what's broken. Fix it. Answer questions.

The implementation is the easy part.

Making it stick after you leave? That's the real job.
```

### Evergreen 11: Your CRM Is a Reflection of Your Sales Process

```
Hot take: Your CRM problems are actually sales process problems.

Messy pipeline? You don't have clear stage definitions.
Low adoption? Your sales process has too many steps.
Bad data? You're not enforcing the behaviors that create good data.

The CRM is a mirror. It reflects whatever you put in front of it.

I can't tell you how many times I've been hired to "fix the CRM" and the real project is fixing how the sales team operates.

Before you blame the tool:

→ Can your reps describe the sales stages without looking at the CRM?
→ Do deal amounts mean anything, or are they placeholders?
→ When a deal moves to the next stage, does something real happen?
→ Can a new rep look at your pipeline and understand what's going on?

If the answer to any of those is "no" — the CRM isn't the problem.

Fix the process. Then the CRM will follow.

I've never seen a great sales process with a bad CRM.

But I've seen plenty of great CRMs masking a terrible sales process.
```

### Evergreen 12: The CRM Buying Mistake

```
The #1 mistake companies make when buying a CRM:

They pick the platform before defining the problem.

"We're a Salesforce shop."
"We use HubSpot because our marketing team chose it."
"Our investor said to use [insert CRM]."

None of those are reasons.

Here's how to actually choose a CRM:

Step 1: Write down your 5 biggest sales visibility problems.
(Not "we need a CRM." What specifically can't you see or do today?)

Step 2: Talk to your reps.
What do they wish they had? What slows them down?

Step 3: Map your must-haves.
Usually: pipeline view, email sync, basic automation, mobile app, integrations with your stack.

Step 4: THEN look at platforms.

For 90% of Series A-C B2B companies, HubSpot does everything you need.

Not because it's the best. Because it's the fastest to implement, easiest to adopt, and most cost-effective until you hit 200+ users.

Salesforce is powerful. But power you can't implement is just expense.

Pick the CRM your team will actually use.

Not the one that looks best in the board deck.
```
